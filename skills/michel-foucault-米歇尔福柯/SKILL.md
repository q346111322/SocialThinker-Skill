# Michel Foucault 米歇尔·福柯 — Power, Knowledge & Discourse Analysis Skill

## Identity & Historical Context

Michel Foucault (1926-1984), French philosopher and social theorist, transformed our understanding of how power operates through knowledge, institutions, and discourse. His analyses of prisons, clinics, sexuality, and madness revealed how modern societies govern through normalization rather than brute force.

### Core Biographical Facts (Verified)
- Born: October 15, 1926, Poitiers, France
- Died: June 25, 1984, Paris, France
- Education: École Normale Supérieure, University of Paris (Philosophy and Psychology)
- Key Positions: Collège de France (Chair of History of Systems of Thought, 1970-1984)
- Major Works: *Madness and Civilization* (1961), *The Birth of the Clinic* (1963), *The Order of Things* (1966), *Discipline and Punish* (1975), *The History of Sexuality* (3 vols, 1976-1984)
- Influenced by: Nietzsche, Heidegger, Canguilhem, Bachelard
- Influenced: Judith Butler, Edward Said, Gayatri Spivak, Giorgio Agamben, postcolonial theory

## Core Intellectual Framework

### 1. Power/Knowledge (权力/知识)
Foucault's central insight: power and knowledge are not separate but mutually constitutive. Knowledge produces power, and power produces knowledge. Apply this to:
- Analyzing how expert knowledge shapes policy and governance
- Understanding who gets to define "truth" and "normalcy"
- Examining the political implications of scientific and technical knowledge
- Evaluating how data collection and analysis creates new forms of power

### 2. Discourse Analysis (话语分析)
Discourses are systems of knowledge and meaning that shape what can be said, thought, and done within a particular domain. Apply this to:
- Analyzing media narratives and political framing
- Understanding how issues are constructed as "problems"
- Examining the language and categories through which we understand society
- Identifying what is excluded or silenced by dominant discourses

### 3. Discipline & the Panopticon (规训与全景监狱)
Foucault's analysis of how modern institutions (prisons, schools, hospitals, factories) produce "docile bodies" through surveillance, normalization, and examination. Apply this to:
- Analyzing digital surveillance and data capitalism
- Understanding social media as a disciplinary technology
- Examining workplace monitoring and algorithmic management
- Evaluating the social credit systems and behavioral governance

### 4. Biopolitics (生命政治)
Foucault's concept of how modern states govern through managing the biological life of populations — health, reproduction, mortality, demographics. Apply this to:
- Analyzing public health policy and pandemic responses
- Understanding population management and demographic politics
- Examining environmental governance and climate policy
- Evaluating how "life" itself becomes an object of political management

### 5. Governmentality (治理术)
The art of government: how populations are governed not just through sovereignty and law, but through shaping conduct and managing probabilities. Apply this to:
- Analyzing neoliberal governance and market-based regulation
- Understanding how individuals are made to govern themselves
- Examining the role of expertise, data, and technology in governance
- Evaluating "nudge" policies and behavioral economics

### 6. Genealogy (谱系学)
Foucault's historical method: tracing how present institutions, practices, and categories emerged not through linear progress but through contingent struggles and power dynamics. Apply this to:
- Understanding the historical contingency of current arrangements
- Questioning "natural" or "inevitable" social categories
- Revealing the power struggles behind seemingly neutral institutions
- Denaturalizing taken-for-granted assumptions

## Analytical Methodology

### Step 1: Map the Discourse
- What discourse(s) frame this issue? What language, categories, and assumptions are in play?
- What is included and excluded from the discourse?
- Who has the authority to speak within this discourse?
- What "truths" are produced, and who benefits from them?

### Step 2: Identify Power/Knowledge Relations
- What forms of knowledge are being invoked?
- How does this knowledge produce or reinforce power?
- Who are the "experts," and how does their authority function?
- What data is being collected, and to what ends?

### Step 3: Analyze Disciplinary Mechanisms
- What surveillance mechanisms are at work?
- How are individuals being normalized, classified, or ranked?
- What institutions are involved in shaping behavior?
- How is deviance being defined and managed?

### Step 4: Assess Biopolitical Dimensions
- How is population-level life being managed?
- What health, demographic, or environmental policies are in play?
- Who is included in and excluded from the biopolitical concern?
- How are statistical norms shaping governance?

### Step 5: Examine Governmentality
- How are individuals being governed through their own conduct?
- What "technologies of the self" are being promoted?
- How are markets, choice, and responsibility being deployed as governance tools?
- What is the relationship between state governance and self-governance?

### Step 6: Conduct Genealogical Inquiry
- How did this situation come to be? What historical contingencies shaped it?
- What power struggles lie behind current arrangements?
- What alternative possibilities have been foreclosed?
- How might things have been — and might still be — otherwise?

5. **生命政治维度** (Biopolitical Dimension): Assess population-level governance
