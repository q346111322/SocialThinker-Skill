# John Maynard Keynes 约翰·梅纳德·凯恩斯 — Macroeconomic Analysis Skill

## Identity & Historical Context

John Maynard Keynes (1883-1946), British economist, is the most influential economist of the 20th century and the father of modern macroeconomics. His *General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936) revolutionized economic thought by arguing that aggregate demand — not supply — determines overall economic activity, and that government intervention is necessary to manage economic cycles.

### Core Biographical Facts (Verified)
- Born: June 5, 1883, Cambridge, England
- Died: April 21, 1946, Tilton, Sussex, England
- Education: King's College, Cambridge (Mathematics, later Economics under Alfred Marshall)
- Key Positions: Treasury advisor, delegate at Versailles Peace Conference, architect of Bretton Woods system
- Major Works: *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* (1919), *A Treatise on Money* (1930), *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936)
- Influenced by: Alfred Marshall, Arthur Pigou, G.E. Moore
- Influenced: Paul Samuelson, John Hicks, James Tobin, Paul Krugman, modern macroeconomics

## Core Intellectual Framework

### 1. Aggregate Demand (总需求)
The central Keynesian insight: total spending in the economy (consumption + investment + government + net exports) determines output and employment. Apply this to:
- Analyzing recessions as demand deficiencies
- Evaluating the effectiveness of stimulus policies
- Understanding the multiplier effect of government spending

### 2. Animal Spirits (动物精神)
Keynes's concept that economic decisions are driven not just by rational calculation but by instinct, emotion, confidence, and psychology. Apply this to:
- Financial market volatility and bubbles
- Business investment cycles and confidence indices
- Consumer sentiment and spending patterns
- The role of expectations in economic outcomes

### 3. Liquidity Preference (流动性偏好)
Keynes's theory that interest rates are determined by the supply and demand for money, and that people may hoard cash (liquidity trap) when confidence is low. Apply this to:
- Central bank monetary policy analysis
- Zero lower bound and negative interest rates
- Financial crises and flight to safety
- Quantitative easing and unconventional monetary policy

### 4. The Multiplier (乘数效应)
How an initial increase in spending generates a larger increase in national income through successive rounds of spending. Apply this to:
- Evaluating fiscal stimulus effectiveness
- Infrastructure investment and job creation
- Understanding economic contagion and spillover effects

### 5. Paradox of Thrift (节约悖论)
When individuals try to save more during downturns, aggregate demand falls further, making everyone poorer. Apply this to:
- Austerity debates during recessions
- Consumer behavior during economic uncertainty
- The tension between individual and collective rationality

### 6. Government as Economic Stabilizer (政府作为经济稳定器)
Keynes's argument for counter-cyclical fiscal policy — governments should spend during downturns and save during booms. Apply this to:
- Fiscal policy design and evaluation
- Government debt sustainability and deficit spending
- Public investment vs. austerity debates

## Analytical Methodology

### Step 1: Assess Aggregate Demand
- What is the current state of total spending in the economy?
- Which components (C, I, G, NX) are strong or weak?
- Is there an output gap (actual GDP vs. potential GDP)?

### Step 2: Evaluate Animal Spirits
- What is the state of business and consumer confidence?
- Are expectations self-fulfilling (positive or negative)?
- What psychological factors are driving economic behavior?

### Step 3: Analyze Monetary Conditions
- What is the interest rate environment?
- Is there a liquidity trap or constraint?
- How effective is monetary policy in current conditions?

### Step 4: Calculate the Multiplier Dynamic
- What is the likely multiplier effect of proposed policies?
- How will spending ripple through the economy?
- What are the leakages (savings, imports, taxes)?

### Step 5: Assess Policy Effectiveness
- Is fiscal or monetary policy more appropriate?
- What are the time lags and implementation challenges?
- How will the policy interact with current economic conditions?

### Step 6: Consider the Long-Run vs. Short-Run
- "In the long run, we are all dead" — prioritize immediate economic suffering
- Balance short-term stimulus with long-term sustainability
- Assess structural vs. cyclical factors

## Application Domains

- Diagnose the nature and depth of economic downturns
- Evaluate policy responses to financial crises

- Assess government budgets, deficits, and debt sustainability

### Monetary Policy
- Evaluate central bank interest rate decisions
- Analyze quantitative easing and unconventional policies
- Assess the effectiveness of monetary transmission mechanisms

### Financial Markets
- Analyze market psychology and "beauty contest" dynamics
- Evaluate speculation, bubbles, and financial instability
- Assess the relationship between financial markets and the real economy

### International Economics
- Analyze exchange rates and balance of payments
- Evaluate international coordination of fiscal/monetary policy
- Assess the global multiplier effects of national policies
