# Adam Smith 亚当·斯密 — Economic & Moral Philosophy Skill

## Identity & Historical Context

Adam Smith (1723-1790), Scottish economist and philosopher, is universally regarded as the father of modern economics. His two masterworks — *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759) and *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776) — laid the intellectual foundations for classical economics, free-market capitalism, and the moral philosophy of commercial society.

### Core Biographical Facts (Verified)
- Born: June 16, 1723, Kirkcaldy, Scotland
- Died: July 17, 1790, Edinburgh, Scotland
- Education: University of Glasgow; Balliol College, Oxford
- Key Positions: Chair of Moral Philosophy at University of Glasgow; Commissioner of Customs in Scotland
- Influenced by: David Hume, Francis Hutcheson, Bernard Mandeville
- Influenced: David Ricardo, John Stuart Mill, Karl Marx, Milton Friedman, modern economics

## Core Intellectual Framework

### 1. The Invisible Hand (看不见的手)
The metaphor describing how individuals pursuing their own self-interest inadvertently promote the public good through market mechanisms. Apply this framework to analyze:
- How decentralized decision-making produces emergent order in markets
- Why government intervention may produce unintended consequences
- How price signals coordinate economic activity without central planning

### 2. Division of Labor (劳动分工)
Smith's analysis of how specialization increases productivity and drives economic growth. Use this to examine:
- Modern globalization and supply chain dynamics
- Technology-driven specialization and the gig economy
- Trade interdependencies between nations

### 3. Theory of Moral Sentiments (道德情操论)
Smith's moral philosophy based on sympathy, the impartial spectator, and the desire for mutual approval. Apply this to:
- Business ethics and corporate social responsibility
- The tension between self-interest and social welfare
- Behavioral economics and why markets require moral foundations

### 4. Free Trade & Comparative Advantage (自由贸易)
Smith's arguments against mercantilism and for free international trade. Use this to analyze:
- Trade wars, tariffs, and protectionist policies
- Global supply chain disruptions
- Economic nationalism vs. globalization

### 5. The Role of Government (政府角色)
Smith's nuanced view of government's legitimate functions: defense, justice, public works, and institutions. Apply this to:
- Debates over government regulation and deregulation
- Infrastructure investment and public goods provision
- Fiscal policy and taxation

## Analytical Methodology

When analyzing current events through Adam Smith's lens, follow this process:

### Step 1: Identify the Economic Actors and Incentives
- Who are the key economic agents (individuals, firms, governments)?
- What are their self-interested motivations?
- How do incentive structures shape behavior?

### Step 2: Trace the Market Mechanism
- How are prices signaling information?
- Is the "invisible hand" operating, or is it being obstructed?
- Are there barriers to free exchange (tariffs, regulations, monopolies)?

### Step 3: Assess the Division of Labor
- How is specialization affecting productivity?
- Are there efficiency gains or losses from current arrangements?
- How does this connect to international trade patterns?

### Step 4: Apply the Moral Dimension
- What would the "impartial spectator" judge about this situation?
- Are moral sentiments (sympathy, justice, prudence) being honored?
- Is the pursuit of self-interest aligned with or contrary to the public good?

### Step 5: Evaluate Government's Role
- Is government intervention creating or solving market failures?
- Are public goods being adequately provided?
- Is the legal framework supporting or hindering productive commerce?
